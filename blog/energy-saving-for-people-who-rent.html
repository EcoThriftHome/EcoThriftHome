Why Renters Often Pay More for Energy

39 40

Here's something that might surprise you: American renters use nearly a third more energy per square foot than homeowners, according to the Energy Information Administration (via Harvard's Joint Center for Housing Studies). Not because renters are careless — but because the system is stacked against them.

41 42

Consider these facts:

43 44
    45
  • 75% of renters live in buildings built before 1990. These older units often have single-pane windows, minimal insulation, and outdated HVAC systems.
    • 46
  • The average 1-bedroom apartment electricity bill runs $102.62/month — that's over $1,200 a year just for electricity (Apartment List, 2026).
    • 47
  • Nearly 50% of all American renter households are "cost-burdened," spending more than 30% of their income on housing, according to the Census Bureau (2024).
    • 48
  • There's a well-documented "split incentive problem": because landlords don't pay the utility bills, they have little reason to upgrade insulation, windows, or appliances. You foot the bill for their inaction.
    • 49
50 51

The split incentive problem is real — but it doesn't mean you're helpless. Your personal electricity use (lighting, electronics, small appliances) can represent 30–50% of your total bill — and that part is entirely under your control.

Free Changes That Cost $0 (But Save Hundreds)

58 59

Before you spend a single dollar, there are several behavioral changes that cost nothing and can collectively save $100–$200 per year. Start here.

60 61

Wash Clothes in Cold Water

62 63

This one blew my mind when I first learned it: 90% of your washing machine's energy goes to heating the water, not running the motor. Switching to cold-water washes saves an estimated $60/year and is equally effective for most everyday loads (ENERGY STAR).

64 65

Cold water is gentler on fabrics, too. Win-win.

66 67

Use Your Thermostat Smarter

68 69

Even without a smart thermostat, manually setting your thermostat back 7–10°F for 8 hours a day (when you're at work or asleep) can save about 10% per year on heating and cooling, according to Energy.gov. That translates to roughly $90+ per year for the average renter.

70 71

Almost half of your energy bill — ~48% — goes to heating and cooling. Small thermostat habits move the needle more than almost anything else.

72 73

Use Window Coverings Strategically

74 75

Close west-facing blinds in summer afternoons to block heat gain. Open south-facing blinds in winter to let in free solar heat. It sounds too simple, but strategic window management is one of the most overlooked no-cost tips in renter energy guides. Solar heat gain through windows can account for 25–30% of cooling loads in summer.

76 77

Run Appliances Off-Peak

78 79

If your utility charges time-of-use rates, running your dishwasher, washing machine, and dryer after 9 PM or before 7 AM can cut electricity costs by 10–30% on those appliances. Check your utility's rate structure — it takes two minutes and could save you real money.

The Phantom Power Problem in Your Apartment

157 158

Here's the counterintuitive tip I promised you: your TV, cable box, gaming console, and phone charger are costing you money right now — even if they're "off."

159 160

This is called "phantom load" or "vampire power," and it's not a small thing. According to the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (via PG&E, 2025), idle/standby power accounts for up to 10% of a typical household's electric bill.

161 162

If your electricity bill averages $103/month, that's about $124/year going to devices that are doing absolutely nothing.

163 164

The biggest offenders in a typical apartment:

165
    166
  • Cable/satellite boxes (often the worst — can draw 15–30W even when "off")
    • 167
  • Gaming consoles (especially older PlayStation and Xbox models in standby mode)
    • 168
  • Older TVs and monitors
    • 169
  • Microwave clock displays
    • 170
  • Phone and laptop chargers left plugged in

Cut Water Heating Costs Without Asking Your Landlord

196 197

Water heating accounts for roughly 15–20% of a typical home's total energy use. Even if your landlord controls the water heater itself, you control how much hot water you use — and that matters a lot.

198 199

200 201

A WaterSense-certified low-flow showerhead is one of the highest-ROI upgrades a renter can make. It costs under $25, installs in under 5 minutes without any tools in most apartments (just twist off the old one, tape the threads, twist on the new one), and you can reinstall the original before you move out.

Turn Down Your Water Heater (If Accessible)

215 216

Many apartments have accessible water heater panels in a closet or utility space. If yours does, check the temperature setting. The EPA recommends 120°F — many units ship at 140°F. Dropping to 120°F can save 4–22% on your water heating costs and reduces scalding risk. Just ask your landlord first if you're unsure.

217 218

Fix Dripping Faucets

219 220

A faucet dripping once per second wastes over 3,000 gallons of water per year. Hot water drips waste electricity, too. Report leaking faucets to your landlord immediately — in most states, they're legally required to fix them, and you shouldn't be paying for that wasted water if it's a metered unit.

Frequently Asked Questions

225 226

Can renters make energy-efficient upgrades without landlord permission?

227

Yes — many of the most impactful energy-saving upgrades require no landlord permission at all. Swapping light bulbs for LEDs, adding a door draft stopper, installing a low-flow showerhead, plugging in a smart power strip, and applying removable window insulation film are all renter-friendly changes you can make today. The key is choosing products that are removable, portable, and non-permanent — and taking them with you when you move.

228 229

How much can renters realistically save on energy each year?

230

Based on data from Energy.gov, ENERGY STAR, and the EPA, a renter who implements a combination of free behavioral changes and a handful of low-cost upgrades can realistically save $300–$700/year. The exact amount depends on your current energy habits, the age of your building, and how many tips you implement. The highest-ROI single change is usually switching to LED bulbs, followed closely by installing a low-flow showerhead.

231 232

What is phantom load, and how much is it costing me?

233

Phantom load (also called standby power or vampire power) is the electricity your devices draw while they're plugged in but not actively in use. According to research from Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, this can account for up to 10% of your total electricity bill. For an average 1-bedroom apartment paying around $103/month in electricity, that's approximately $124/year for devices sitting idle. A smart power strip ($25–$30) or smart plug with energy monitoring ($15–$20) can eliminate most of this waste.

234 235

Are there free energy-saving resources available to renters?

236

Absolutely. Many utility companies offer free energy-saving products — including LED bulbs, low-flow showerheads, and faucet aerators — specifically targeting renters and low-income households. Check your utility company's website or call their customer service line and ask about free efficiency kits or rebate programs. Additionally, some states offer community solar programs where renters can subscribe to local solar energy at a discount — no installation required, no landlord permission needed. The Database of State Incentives for Renewables & Efficiency (DSIRE) at dsireusa.org lists available programs by state.
