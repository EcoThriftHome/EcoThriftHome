Heating and cooling account for nearly half of the average American home's energy bill — roughly $900 per year according to the U.S. Energy Information Administration. That makes your thermostat schedule one of the highest-leverage dials you have. Yet most smart thermostats ship from the box running a single, unchanging temperature around the clock, leaving serious savings on the table.

This guide cuts through the marketing noise and tells you exactly — degree by degree, hour by hour — what the data says about which schedules save the most money. No guesswork, no vague "up to" promises.

Key Takeaway: Setting back your thermostat 7–10°F for just 8 hours a day can trim your annual heating and cooling costs by around 10%, and stacking a sleep setback on top of an away setback can push total savings toward $180 per year for a typical U.S. household.

Why Setback Schedules Work (The Physics in 60 Seconds)

Your HVAC system doesn't struggle harder to recover from a setback temperature than it saves during the setback period — that's a persistent myth. Heat transfer between your home and the outdoors follows Newton's Law of Cooling: the greater the temperature difference between inside and outside, the faster heat flows in or out. When you lower the indoor temperature in winter, your home loses heat to the outside more slowly, meaning your furnace runs less. The brief recovery period at the end of a setback uses far less energy than the continuous trickle lost maintaining a higher temperature for hours.

The math heavily favors setbacks, and every major energy authority agrees.

"You can save as much as 10% a year on heating and cooling by simply turning your thermostat back 7–10°F for 8 hours a day from its normal setting."

— U.S. Department of Energy, Energy Saver: Programmable Thermostats

The Four Schedule Periods That Matter

Smart thermostats divide the day into up to four periods. Most households only program two or three, leaving money on the table. Here's how each period should be configured for maximum savings:

1. Wake (6:00 AM – 8:00 AM)

This is your comfort anchor. Set the thermostat to your preferred comfort temperature — typically 68–70°F in winter and 76–78°F in summer. The key is using your thermostat's pre-conditioning feature (sometimes called "early start" or "smart recovery") so the home reaches the target temperature exactly when you wake up, not after a 30-minute warmup period. This avoids the temptation to crank the heat, which wastes energy.

2. Away (8:00 AM – 5:00 PM)

This is the single biggest savings window. Most households are empty for 7–9 hours on workdays. The DOE-recommended setback here is aggressive:

  • Winter: Drop to 60–62°F. Every degree below your comfort setting saves approximately 1% on your heating bill.
  • Summer: Raise to 82–85°F. Every degree above your comfort setting saves approximately 3% on your cooling bill (cooling savings per degree are higher because modern air conditioners must work against the full heat load).

If you have pets, note that most breeds are comfortable down to 60°F; consult your vet if you have specific concerns, but 62°F is a safe floor for the vast majority of households.

3. Home Evening (5:00 PM – 10:00 PM)

Return to your comfort temperature. Use geofencing if your schedule is irregular — the thermostat starts pre-conditioning when your phone's GPS shows you're 10–15 minutes away, avoiding both an empty heated/cooled home and a cold/hot arrival.

4. Sleep (10:00 PM – 6:00 AM)

This period is consistently underutilized. Most people sleep better in a cooler environment, and the DOE data confirms the savings are real:

  • Winter: Set to 65–67°F. A good duvet eliminates comfort loss entirely, and the 8-hour window generates substantial savings.
  • Summer: 74–76°F works for most sleepers, though this is the period where personal comfort varies most. Even a 2-degree raise cuts overnight cooling costs meaningfully.

Seasonal Tuning: Summer vs. Winter Settings

Your thermostat should run on two distinct seasonal profiles. Many homeowners set a schedule once in November and forget it, running the same setpoints in July. That's a mistake — cooling economics are different from heating economics.

Winter baseline (heating-dominated climates): The DOE comfort sweet spot is 68°F when awake and active at home. Each degree you lower the thermostat below 68°F saves roughly 1% on your heating bill. A 10°F away setback to 58°F can save around 10% on your total heating costs for that period.

Summer baseline (cooling-dominated climates): ENERGY STAR recommends 78°F as the comfort baseline when home. The savings rate per degree is higher in cooling mode — approximately 3% per degree — because air conditioners must continuously fight solar gain, infiltration heat, and internal loads simultaneously. Raising the setpoint to 85°F during an 8-hour away period in a hot climate can shave 20% off your summer cooling bill for that window.

If you live in a mixed climate (cold winters, hot summers), program both seasonal profiles and switch them manually in spring and fall, or use a thermostat with automatic seasonal changeover.

Geofencing vs. Fixed Schedule: Which Saves More?

Geofencing wins for households with irregular routines — it eliminates the "I forgot to adjust the thermostat" problem and prevents conditioning an empty home. Studies from Nest's internal data suggest geofencing users save an additional 5–10% compared to those who forget to manually switch to away mode.

However, a well-programmed fixed schedule beats geofencing for households with consistent 9-to-5 routines. Why? Fixed schedules can leverage pre-conditioning — the thermostat starts recovery 30 minutes before you arrive, using off-peak energy pricing in some utility markets, and ensuring the home is comfortable the moment you walk in without any delay.

The best approach for most households: use a fixed schedule as the foundation and enable geofencing as an override for unexpected early arrivals or late departures.

Common Scheduling Mistakes That Cost You Money

  • Setting the recovery ramp too aggressive. If you set "wake" to 70°F but the sleep period runs at 60°F, a sudden 10-degree recovery in 15 minutes forces your furnace to run at maximum capacity. Instead, start the recovery 45–60 minutes before wake time and let the system ramp gently.
  • Forgetting weekends. Most smart thermostats allow separate weekend schedules. If you sleep in and stay home on Saturdays and Sundays, don't use your weekday away schedule — you'll be conditioning a full house to away temperatures unnecessarily.
  • Ignoring the "hold" override. Using a permanent temperature hold defeats the entire schedule. Use temporary holds (2–4 hours maximum) and always return to schedule mode.
  • Skipping the sleep period in summer. Overnight temperatures drop in most U.S. climates. If outdoor temps fall below 72°F, using a ventilation or fan-only mode instead of air conditioning for even part of the night generates real savings.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many degrees should I set back my thermostat to save money?

The U.S. Department of Energy recommends setting back your thermostat 7–10°F from your normal setting for 8 hours per day. This single change can save roughly 10% per year on heating and cooling costs. Going beyond 10°F provides diminishing returns and can stress your HVAC system in very cold climates.

Is it cheaper to leave the thermostat at one temperature all day?

No — this is one of the most persistent energy myths. The physics are clear: the greater the temperature difference between your home and the outside, the faster heat transfers across your walls, windows, and roof. Holding a higher indoor temperature in winter means continuously fighting a steeper heat-loss gradient. Setback schedules consistently save more money for every home type studied by the DOE.

Does a smart thermostat pay for itself?

For most households, yes. ENERGY STAR estimates certified smart thermostats save an average of $50 per year on a minimum basis. Households that fully optimize their schedules using the strategies in this guide typically see $100–$180 per year in savings, putting payback between 1 and 2 years for mid-range models and under 6 months for a $45 programmable unit.

What is the best temperature schedule for summer?

Set 78°F when home and awake, 82–85°F when away, and 80°F when sleeping. Each degree you raise the setpoint in cooling mode saves approximately 3% on your cooling bill. Running ceiling fans allows you to raise the "feels like" temperature by 4°F without discomfort, effectively extending the savings window.

Should I use geofencing or a fixed schedule?

Use a fixed schedule as your baseline if your routine is consistent — it allows pre-conditioning, which improves comfort and can leverage time-of-use utility rates. Enable geofencing as an override for days when you leave early or come home late. The combination outperforms either method used alone for most households.
