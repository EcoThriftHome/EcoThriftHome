How to Reduce AC Bills in Summer: 12 Proven Tips That Actually Work

Air conditioning accounts for nearly 17% of the average U.S. home's electricity bill. These 12 data-backed strategies can cut that number significantly — some cost nothing at all.

Summer electricity bills have a way of sneaking up on you. You crank up the AC, enjoy a comfortable July, and then wince at a bill that's double what it was in May. The good news: a significant chunk of that cost is preventable. You don't need a full HVAC overhaul or a solar panel system to make a real dent. You need the right information and a handful of targeted actions.

We've pulled together 12 tips grounded in data from the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE), ENERGY STAR, and the EPA. Some are free and take five minutes. Others require a modest investment that pays back in a single season. Let's dig in.

Key Takeaway: Combining thermostat discipline, air sealing, shade strategies, and regular maintenance can realistically cut your summer AC costs by 20–40% without sacrificing comfort.

1. Set Your Thermostat to 78°F — and Mean It

The single highest-impact, zero-cost change you can make is raising your thermostat setpoint. The DOE's official recommendation for summer cooling is 78°F (26°C) when you're home and awake. Every degree you lower it below that adds roughly 3% to your cooling bill. If you've been cruising at 72°F, that's an 18% premium you're paying for six extra degrees.

That might sound like a lot to ask, but pairing 78°F with ceiling fans (see Tip 5) makes it feel considerably cooler. The wind-chill effect bridges the gap.

"You can save as much as 10% a year on heating and cooling by simply turning your thermostat back 7°–10°F for 8 hours a day from its normal setting."

— U.S. Department of Energy, Energy Saver Guide

2. Use a Programmable or Smart Thermostat

Manual thermostat discipline is hard to maintain every single day. A programmable or smart thermostat does it automatically. Set it to raise to 85–88°F when the house is empty and cool back down 30 minutes before you return. ENERGY STAR-certified smart thermostats save an average of $50 per year on cooling alone — and many utility companies offer rebates that bring the upfront cost close to zero.

3. Replace Your AC Filter Every 1–3 Months

A clogged air filter is one of the most overlooked energy wasters in any home. When your filter is dirty, the blower motor has to work harder to push air through, which increases electricity consumption by up to 15% according to DOE data. During peak summer use, check your filter monthly. A standard 1-inch filter typically needs replacement every 30–90 days depending on pets, dust levels, and runtime hours.

Filters are cheap — a pack of six quality filters runs about $20. Skipping a monthly filter check can cost you far more than that in wasted electricity.

4. Seal Air Leaks Around Windows and Doors

The EPA estimates that air sealing and insulation can save homeowners up to 15% on heating and cooling costs — or about 11% of total energy bills. Common leakage points include window frames, door thresholds, electrical outlets on exterior walls, and attic hatches. A $5 tube of weatherstripping caulk or a $10 door sweep is one of the best returns on investment in home energy efficiency.

Do a quick DIY audit: on a hot, windy day, hold your hand near window edges and door frames. Feel warm air coming in? That's cooled air escaping and hot air infiltrating — both costing you money simultaneously.

5. Run Ceiling Fans on the Correct Summer Setting

Ceiling fans don't actually cool air — they cool people by creating a wind-chill effect. That means they're only useful when someone is in the room. But when used correctly, they allow you to raise the thermostat by 4°F with no reduction in perceived comfort, per ENERGY STAR data.

The often-missed detail: in summer, your ceiling fan should spin counterclockwise (when viewed from below) to push air straight down and create that cooling breeze. Most fans have a small switch on the motor housing to change direction.

6. Block Solar Heat Gain With Window Treatments

South- and west-facing windows can contribute 10–25% of a home's cooling load during summer afternoons, according to DOE research. Solar heat gain through unshaded glass is essentially a free heat source — except it's the kind you're paying to remove with your AC.

Cellular (honeycomb) shades, reflective window film, and exterior shading like awnings or trees are all effective. Cellular shades alone can reduce heat gain by up to 60% on single-pane windows. Exterior shading is even more effective because it blocks heat before it ever enters the glass.

7. Schedule Annual Professional AC Maintenance

A professional tune-up typically costs $75–$150 and includes coil cleaning, refrigerant level checks, electrical connection tightening, and airflow testing. A well-maintained system runs 5–15% more efficiently than a neglected one. If your unit is low on refrigerant or has dirty evaporator coils, it may be working twice as hard to deliver half the cooling — you'd never know without a pro checking it.

Schedule it in spring before peak season hits and pricing goes up. Many HVAC companies offer maintenance agreements that include priority scheduling and parts discounts.

8. Insulate Your Attic — It's the Biggest Thermal Weak Point

Heat radiates down from a sun-baked roof through the attic into your living space all day long. The DOE estimates that upgrading attic insulation to the recommended level (R-38 to R-60 in most U.S. climate zones) can save 10–50% on total heating and cooling costs. This is a larger project, but it's also one of the highest-ROI home energy investments you can make, with payback periods as short as 3–5 years.

If a full insulation upgrade isn't in the budget right now, start by ensuring your attic hatch is insulated and weatherstripped — it's a surprisingly common energy leak point.

9. Cook Strategically to Reduce Indoor Heat Load

Your oven generates significant heat — running it for an hour can raise kitchen temperature by 5–10°F and force your AC to work harder. On hot days, shift cooking to early morning, use the microwave or outdoor grill, or rely on no-cook meals. This isn't just comfort advice — it measurably reduces the heat your AC has to remove, cutting runtime and energy use.

10. Use Exhaust Fans to Purge Heat After Cooking and Showering

Bathroom and kitchen exhaust fans remove hot, humid air directly from your home. Running them for 15–20 minutes after cooking or showering expels heat and moisture before they spread to the rest of the house. Humidity makes the same temperature feel hotter, so reducing indoor moisture levels lets your AC maintain comfort more efficiently.

11. Upgrade to an ENERGY STAR Certified AC If Yours Is Over 10 Years Old

Central AC units installed before 2015 may have a SEER (Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio) rating as low as 10. Modern ENERGY STAR certified systems start at SEER 15 and can reach SEER 20+. Replacing a SEER 10 unit with a SEER 20 unit cuts your cooling energy use nearly in half. This is a major investment, but worth calculating if your unit is aging, requiring frequent repairs, or running constantly without reaching setpoint.

12. Use a Whole-House Fan for Night Cooling

In climates where nights cool down significantly (generally below 70°F), a whole-house fan is a powerful tool. Installed in the ceiling, it pulls cool outdoor air through open windows and exhausts hot attic air — dropping indoor temperatures 5–10°F in under an hour while using only 10–15% of the electricity of central AC. Many homeowners in temperate climates use whole-house fans as their primary evening cooling strategy, running AC only during the hottest afternoon hours.

Frequently Asked Questions

What temperature should I set my AC to in summer to save money?

The U.S. Department of Energy recommends setting your thermostat to 78°F (26°C) when you're home and raising it to 85°F when you're away. Each degree you raise the thermostat saves approximately 3% on your cooling bill.

How much can a programmable thermostat save on AC bills?

According to ENERGY STAR, a properly programmed smart thermostat can save homeowners around $50–$180 per year on heating and cooling combined, with cooling savings typically making up the larger share in hot climates.
