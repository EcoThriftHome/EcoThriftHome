How Much Does It Cost to Run a Dryer — And 8 Ways to Cut That Bill

Your clothes dryer is one of the top 5 energy hogs in a typical home. Here's exactly what it costs you per load, per year, and how to shrink that number fast.

$0.80Average cost per load
~$200Typical annual dryer cost
Up to 30%Savings with smart habits

Let's be honest: the dryer is one of those appliances we never think about until something goes wrong. You toss in a wet load, press start, and walk away. But that casual routine adds up fast — and for most households, the dryer is responsible for 4–6% of total home electricity use. That puts it squarely in the same league as your refrigerator and dishwasher.

In this post, we'll break down the real numbers — exactly how much your dryer costs per load and per year — and then give you eight practical, evidence-backed strategies to cut that bill significantly. No gadgets required for most of them.

Key Takeaway: The average American household spends roughly $175–$220 per year running an electric dryer, but with a few habit changes and one or two low-cost tools, you can realistically trim that by $50–$80 annually.

How Much Electricity Does a Dryer Actually Use?

A standard residential electric dryer draws between 4,000 and 5,000 watts (4–5 kW) while it runs. Most cycles last between 45 and 60 minutes, which means each load consumes roughly 4 to 5 kilowatt-hours (kWh) of electricity.

The U.S. Energy Information Administration (EIA) pegs the national average residential electricity rate at $0.17 per kWh as of early 2026, though rates vary widely — from about $0.10/kWh in Louisiana to over $0.30/kWh in Hawaii and parts of California and New England. Use your own rate from your utility bill for the most accurate figure.

Here's the quick math at $0.17/kWh:

  • Per load: 4.5 kWh × $0.17 = ~$0.77
  • Per week (5 loads): ~$3.83
  • Per year (260 loads): ~$199

Gas dryers are considerably cheaper to operate because natural gas costs less per unit of heat than electricity. A gas dryer typically costs $0.25–$0.35 per load in fuel, putting annual costs around $65–$90 for the same usage. The trade-off is a higher upfront purchase price and the need for a gas hookup.

8 Evidence-Based Ways to Cut Your Dryer Bill

1. Spin Clothes Faster in the Washer First

This is the single highest-leverage tip with zero ongoing cost. Your washer's spin cycle removes moisture mechanically, which requires far less energy than evaporating it thermally in a dryer. Upgrading from a low-spin cycle (~600 RPM) to a high-spin cycle (~1,200 RPM) can remove enough extra moisture to cut dryer time by 10–15 minutes per load. If your washer has an "extra spin" or "high spin" option, use it every time. Over a year of five weekly loads, that alone can save you $20–$30.

2. Clean the Lint Trap Before Every Single Load

This sounds obvious, but a partially clogged lint trap restricts airflow, forcing the heating element and motor to work harder and run longer. The U.S. Fire Administration estimates that dryer lint fires cause roughly 2,900 house fires per year — so this habit protects your wallet and your home. Clear it before every load, no exceptions.

3. Clean the Dryer Vent Duct Annually

The lint trap only catches a fraction of the lint your dryer produces. The rest accumulates in the exhaust duct. A clogged duct can add 20–30% to your drying time and creates a serious fire hazard. Use a dryer vent brush kit (typically $15–$25 on Amazon) to clean the full duct length once a year. It takes about 20 minutes and can noticeably restore drying performance.

4. Use Moisture-Sensing Mode, Not Timed Dry

Most dryers made in the last decade have a moisture sensor built in. When activated, the dryer stops automatically once clothes reach a set dryness level instead of running for a fixed time. Studies by Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory have found that sensor-equipped dryers save 10–15% of energy per cycle compared to timed cycles. If your dryer has this feature, make sure it's selected — and check that the sensor bars (usually two metal strips inside the drum) are clean. A quick wipe with rubbing alcohol every few months keeps them accurate.

5. Dry Full Loads — But Not Overloaded Ones

Running a half-empty dryer is almost as wasteful as overloading it. A dryer uses essentially the same startup energy whether the drum is 30% full or 80% full, so waiting to accumulate a full load is the smarter play. On the flip side, an overloaded drum restricts airflow, prevents clothes from tumbling properly, and stretches cycle times significantly. Aim for a drum that's about 75–80% full — snug but not stuffed.

6. Sort Loads by Fabric Weight

Mixing heavy towels with lightweight t-shirts is a recipe for inefficiency. The light items finish drying 20 minutes before the towels do, but the whole load keeps running. Drying similar fabrics together means the dryer can stop on time for everything. It takes an extra 30 seconds during laundry sorting and costs nothing.

7. Use Dryer Balls Instead of Dryer Sheets

Wool dryer balls tumble with your laundry, physically separating clothes so hot air can circulate more freely. Multiple independent tests (including from Consumer Reports) have found they can reduce drying time by 10–25% compared to using no additive — or versus dryer sheets, which can coat fabric fibers and slightly reduce absorbency over time. A set of three to six dryer balls costs $10–$20 and lasts for hundreds of loads.

8. Air-Dry Strategically

You don't have to go full clothesline-convert to see real savings. Identifying your biggest, heaviest loads — bed sheets, towels, jeans — and air-drying those on an indoor drying rack or outdoor line even just once or twice a week can cut your dryer usage by 30–40%. Should You Upgrade to a Heat Pump Dryer?

Heat pump dryers are the ENERGY STAR darlings of the laundry room right now — and for good reason. Instead of generating heat from a resistive element and venting it outside, they recycle hot air in a closed loop, using roughly half the electricity of a conventional electric dryer per load.

The math on upgrading is straightforward: at $0.17/kWh and five loads per week, you'd save roughly $110 per year over a standard electric dryer. A mid-range heat pump dryer runs $800–$1,200, so the payback period is 7–11 years — longer than many people keep appliances, but worth factoring in if your current dryer is already aging out.

Also worth noting: the Inflation Reduction Act currently provides a federal tax credit of up to 30% (capped at $150) for qualified heat pump dryers, and many utilities offer additional rebates. Check the ENERGY STAR Rebate Finder at energystar.gov to see what's available in your area before you buy.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much electricity does a dryer use per load?

A standard electric dryer uses about 4–5 kWh per load (roughly 45–60 minutes). At the U.S. average electricity rate of $0.17/kWh, that works out to approximately $0.68–$0.85 per load.

How much does it cost to run a dryer per year?

If you run 5 loads per week, a standard electric dryer costs roughly $175–$220 per year. A gas dryer running on natural gas costs significantly less — around $60–$90 per year for the same usage.

Does using a lower heat setting save energy?

Yes. Using a lower heat setting extends cycle time slightly but can reduce peak energy draw and protects fabrics. The bigger energy win is using a moisture-sensing feature so the dryer stops automatically rather than running a timed cycle.

How much can I save by air-drying clothes?

Air-drying even half your loads could cut your dryer energy bill by up to 50%, saving $85–$110 per year for an average household.

Does cleaning the lint trap really save money?

Yes. A clogged lint trap forces the dryer to work harder and run longer. The U.S. Fire Administration and DOE both recommend cleaning the lint trap before every load to maintain efficiency and reduce fire risk.
