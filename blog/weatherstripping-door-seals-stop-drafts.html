Weatherstripping & Door Seals: The Cheapest Way to Stop Drafts and Lower Your Energy Bill

Your doors might be costing you $150–$300 a year through gaps you've never noticed. Here's how to find them, fix them yourself, and keep that money in your pocket.

Why Leaky Doors Are a Bigger Problem Than You Think

Air leakage is one of the most underrated energy problems in residential homes. According to the U.S. Department of Energy, the average American home loses 25–40% of its heating and cooling energy through air leaks — and doors are among the top offenders. The gap under a standard exterior door that hasn't been sealed is roughly equivalent to a two-inch square hole in your wall. You wouldn't leave a hole like that in your wall, but most of us walk past it every day without thinking twice.

The good news is that weatherstripping and door sweeps are among the highest-return investments in home energy efficiency. Materials are inexpensive, installation is genuinely beginner-friendly, and the payback period is often measured in weeks rather than years. This guide walks you through exactly what you need, how to diagnose your specific problem, and what to buy without overspending.

How to Diagnose a Leaky Door (3 Reliable Tests)

Before you buy anything, confirm you actually have a draft problem and find exactly where the air is escaping. Don't skip this step — it saves you from replacing weatherstripping that doesn't need replacing.

  • The incense or smoke pencil test: On a cold or windy day, hold a lit stick of incense or a smoke pencil about an inch from the edges of your closed door. Slowly move it along the top, sides, and bottom. Any wavering or bending of the smoke indicates air movement. Mark those spots with tape.
  • The dollar bill test: Close the door on a dollar bill. Try to slide it out. If it pulls free easily with no resistance, the seal at that point is inadequate. Repeat this at multiple points around the door perimeter.
  • The daylight test: On a sunny day, turn off the lights in the room and look for daylight seeping around the door frame. Any light you can see is a gap that air — and your money — is flowing through.

Pay particular attention to the bottom of the door (often the biggest gap), the latch side (which gets the most flex from opening and closing), and the top corners. These are the spots most likely to show wear on an older door.

Understanding the Four Types of Weatherstripping

Not all weatherstripping is built for the same job. Using the wrong type is a common mistake that leads to early failure or poor sealing. Here's a plain-English breakdown:

1. V-Strip (Tension Seal)

V-strip is a folded strip of metal or plastic that creates a spring-like seal. It's installed inside the door stop channel and works by springing outward to fill gaps. This is the most durable option for the sides and top of frequently used doors. It handles friction well, doesn't compress permanently over time, and can last 10–15 years. It's also nearly invisible once installed. This is the type we recommend for exterior doors used multiple times per day.

2. Foam Tape

Self-adhesive foam tape is the cheapest and easiest to install — you peel and stick it to the door stop. It compresses to fill gaps when the door is closed. The downside is durability: foam tape on a busy exterior door typically lasts 1–3 years before it compresses permanently and loses effectiveness. It's a better fit for windows, infrequently used doors like a garage entry, or as a quick seasonal fix while you plan a more permanent solution.

3. Door Sweeps and Door Bottoms

The bottom of the door is almost always the biggest source of air infiltration. A door sweep is a strip of metal with a flexible rubber, vinyl, or brush flap that attaches to the interior face of the door bottom. An automatic door bottom is more sophisticated — it uses a spring mechanism to drop the seal when the door closes and lift it when it opens, preventing the sweep from dragging on carpets. For most homes, a quality door sweep costing $15–$30 solves the problem. If you have thick carpet or a door that catches, an automatic door bottom is worth the upgrade.

4. Door Thresholds and Threshold Seals

The threshold is the strip on the floor that your door closes against. Many thresholds have an integrated rubber or vinyl gasket that creates the seal at the bottom. If your threshold seal is cracked or crushed, you can often replace just the vinyl insert rather than the whole threshold — this is a $10–$20 fix that takes five minutes. Check your threshold carefully before assuming you need a full replacement.

Step-by-Step: How to Weatherstrip an Exterior Door

Here's the full process from start to finish. Budget about 45–60 minutes and gather these tools before you start: measuring tape, scissors or utility knife, a hammer, and possibly a screwdriver depending on your door sweep type.

  1. Remove old weatherstripping completely. Peel off foam tape residue with a plastic scraper. Pull out old V-strip. Clean all surfaces with rubbing alcohol and let dry. Adhesive sticks much better to a clean, dry surface — this step directly affects how long your new seals last.
  2. Measure the door perimeter. Measure the height of both sides (hinge side and latch side) and the width of the top. Add 10% for cutting margin. Measure the door width again for your door sweep.
  3. Install V-strip on the sides and top. Cut V-strip to length. Open the door and press the strip into the door stop channel with the open side of the V facing outward (toward the outside when the door closes). Use a block of wood and hammer to tap it firmly into the groove if it's the metal type. For self-adhesive versions, peel and press firmly. Do the latch side first, then the hinge side, then the top.
  4. Install the door sweep. Close the door and mark a line on the interior face at the bottom edge. Position the door sweep so the rubber flap just grazes the threshold when the door is closed — you want light contact, not enough drag to make closing difficult. Drill pilot holes and screw it in place. Trim to width if needed.
  5. Check the threshold seal. If the rubber gasket in the threshold is damaged, pry out the old insert and press in a new one. They're typically sold by the foot at hardware stores for about $3–$5 per foot.
  6. Test your work. Run the incense test again. The smoke should hang straight down with no movement anywhere around the door frame.

How Many Doors Should You Prioritize?

Start with exterior doors that lead directly outside, in order of how drafty they are based on your diagnosis. Front doors and back doors are almost always the priority. Garage entry doors are often neglected but can be significant because garages are typically unconditioned space — air leaking through that door is almost as bad as a door opening directly to the outside. Sliding glass doors have their own sealing system (pile weatherstripping), which we'll cover in a separate post.

Interior doors generally don't need weatherstripping unless you're trying to soundproof a room or isolate a home office. Save your budget for the exterior.

The Real Numbers: What to Expect in Savings

Let's be realistic about what weatherstripping alone will do for your energy bill. The DOE's 10–20% air sealing savings figure applies to a comprehensive air sealing project covering doors, windows, attic hatches, electrical outlets, and other penetrations throughout the whole house. Doing just your exterior doors will move the needle, but probably by 3–8% on its own, depending on how leaky your doors were to start.

For a household with a $1,500 annual heating and cooling bill, that's $45–$120 per year from doors alone. With a total material cost of $50–$120 for two or three exterior doors, you're looking at a payback period of one to two heating seasons. That's a genuinely good return on a Saturday afternoon's work.

The non-financial benefit is also real: a properly sealed door means more even temperatures, less cold-floor misery in winter, and fewer allergy triggers from infiltrating outdoor air. These quality-of-life improvements are hard to put a number on but easy to notice.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much money can weatherstripping actually save me?

The U.S. Department of Energy estimates that air sealing and weatherstripping together can reduce heating and cooling costs by 10–20%. For a typical household spending $1,500 per year on heating and cooling, that's $150–$300 in annual savings. Weatherstripping itself, which costs $20–$60 per door, usually pays for itself within a single heating season.

How do I know if my door needs new weatherstripping?

Three reliable tests: (1) Hold a lit stick of incense near the door edges on a windy day — if the smoke wavers, air is leaking. (2) Slip a dollar bill into the closed door; if it slides out easily, the seal is worn. (3) Look for visible daylight around the door frame. Any of these signs means it's time to replace or add weatherstripping.

What is the best type of weatherstripping for an exterior door?

For exterior doors, V-strip (tension seal) on the sides and top of the door frame combined with a door sweep or automatic door bottom on the threshold is the most durable and effective combination. V-strip is self-adhesive, long-lasting, and handles the friction of a frequently used door well. Foam tape is cheaper but wears out faster on high-traffic doors.

Can I install weatherstripping myself, or do I need a professional?

This is a genuine DIY project. Most weatherstripping products require only scissors, a utility knife, and sometimes a hammer or screwdriver. A full exterior door can be sealed in under an hour. No special skills needed — just clean, dry surfaces and careful measurement.

Does weatherstripping work in summer too, or just winter?

It works year-round. In winter it keeps heated air in and cold air out. In summer it does the reverse, reducing the load on your air conditioner by keeping cooled air inside and hot, humid outdoor air from seeping in. The energy savings are real in both seasons.
